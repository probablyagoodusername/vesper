We are going to practice centering prayer. This is one of the oldest forms of Christian contemplation — rooted in the Desert Fathers and Mothers of the fourth century, refined by the author of The Cloud of Unknowing in the fourteenth, and revived in our time by Thomas Keating and Basil Pennington.

It is, in essence, very simple. You sit in God's presence. And you consent to be there.

That's it.

There is no insight to achieve. No emotion to manufacture. No problem to solve. This is not thinking about God. It is being with God. The way you might sit with an old friend in comfortable silence — not because there's nothing to say, but because the relationship has gone deeper than words.

[15s pause]

The psalmist writes: "Be still, and know that I am God." Psalm forty-six, verse ten.

Be still. Not "be productive." Not "be insightful." Be still.

[15s pause]

Here is how the practice works.

First, choose a sacred word. This word is not a mantra. You're not repeating it to create an effect. It's a symbol — a gesture of your intention to consent to God's presence and action within you.

The word should be short. One or two syllables. It should resonate with your desire for God. Traditional choices include: Jesus. Abba. Come. Peace. Mercy. Love. Stillness. Here.

Take a moment now. Let a word rise. Don't overthink it. The word that comes... is fine.

[15s pause]

Good. Hold that word lightly. Like a feather in an open palm.

[10s pause]

Now, here's the practice. I'll explain it simply, and then we'll enter the silence together.

You sit with eyes closed. You introduce your sacred word gently — not as a repetition, but as a way of saying to God: "I am here. I consent to your presence."

Thoughts will come. They always come. Sensations, memories, plans, emotions, itches, fantasies, worries, brilliant ideas, grocery lists... they will all come. This is normal. This is not failure. This is being human.

When you notice you've been carried away by a thought — and you will notice, because something in you is deeper than the thoughts — you gently return to your sacred word. Not with frustration. Not with self-judgment. Just... gently. The way you'd pick up a child who's wandered off. No scolding. Just a gentle return.

Thomas Keating described it this way: thoughts in centering prayer are like boats going by on a river. You're sitting on the bank. The boats will pass. You don't need to get on any of them. And when you realize you've somehow ended up on a boat — you simply step back onto the bank.

Your sacred word is how you step back onto the bank.

[15s pause]

That's all there is. Consent. Silence. Thoughts. Return. Consent again.

The "goal" — if there is one — is not the absence of thought. It is the willingness to return. Every time you return to your sacred word, you are practicing surrender. You are practicing trust. You are saying, again and again: I choose you over my own noise.

Elijah knew this. In First Kings, chapter nineteen, he stood on the mountain waiting for God. And a great wind came — but God was not in the wind. And an earthquake came — but God was not in the earthquake. And a fire came — but God was not in the fire.

And then... a still small voice. Some translations say "a sound of sheer silence."

God was in the silence.

You don't have to fight the wind. You just have to wait for the silence.
