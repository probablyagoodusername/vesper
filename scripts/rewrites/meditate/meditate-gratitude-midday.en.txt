"This is the day that the Lord has made; let us rejoice and be glad in it." Psalm one-eighteen, verse twenty-four.

Not tomorrow. Not the day you're planning for. Not the perfect day you keep waiting for — the one where everything is in order, where the inbox is empty, where you finally feel on top of things. That day isn't coming. This one is here. With all its interruptions and small annoyances and unremarkable moments and hidden graces — this is the day.

[15s pause]

We're going to spend these next few minutes doing something inspired by the Ignatian Examen — a practice that Ignatius of Loyola taught nearly five hundred years ago. He considered it the most important prayer of the day, more important even than the Mass. And he placed it at midday and evening — not morning, but the middle and end of the day. Because this isn't a prayer about planning or hoping or setting intentions. It's a prayer about paying attention to what has already happened. Looking back over the hours you've already lived and asking: where was God in this? Where was the grace I missed because I was too busy to notice?

Ignatius used two words. Consolation — the moments where your heart expanded, where something felt right, where you felt alive or grateful or connected, even briefly. And desolation — the moments where your heart contracted, where you felt drained or anxious or hollow or far from God. Both are important. Both reveal something. We'll look for both.

[15s pause]

Let your mind travel back to the beginning of this day. The very first moments. The alarm, or the light through the window, or the sounds that woke you. Don't rush. Move through the morning slowly, like paging through a photo album. Not analyzing. Just... remembering.

[15s pause]

Start with the very first good thing. It might have been tiny — so small that you didn't even register it at the time. The warmth of the bed before you got out of it. The first sip of something hot. The feeling of water on your face. A moment of quiet before the noise started. Light coming through a window at a particular angle.

Let it come to you. Don't force it. Just scan gently, like running your hand over fabric, feeling for the texture that catches.

[20s pause]

When you find it, stay there. Don't move on yet. Feel it again — not just the memory, but the sensation. The warmth. The taste. The quality of the light. Let your body re-experience it.

Where does it register? Where in your body do you feel the echo of that good thing? Warmth in your chest? A softening in your shoulders? A settling in your stomach? Something around the eyes?

[15s pause]

This was a consolation. A moment of grace. Small, ordinary, easy to miss — and God was in it. Not dramatically. Not with thunder and angels. Just... quietly present in the warmth of a cup, the kindness of the light, the mercy of a body that woke up one more time.

Silently, simply: thank you.

[20s pause]

Now think of a person. Someone you encountered this morning or early afternoon — in person, on a call, over a message, even in passing. Someone who made the day a little better. A little warmer. A little less alone.

It might be someone who listened. Someone who made you laugh. A coworker who handled something so you didn't have to. A stranger who was patient. A child who said something absurd and true. Someone who simply showed up and did their job with grace, making your life easier without knowing it.

See their face. Remember the specific moment — what was said, or the tone of voice, or the expression, or the simple fact of their presence.

[20s pause]

What would this day have been without them? Not dramatically worse, maybe. But emptier. Thinner. Less human. That person is a gift — an unearned, undeserved, ordinary gift. They didn't owe you that kindness, that patience, that laugh. But they gave it. And you received it, probably without even thinking about it.

Think about it now.

[15s pause]

Silently: thank you. For them. That they exist. That your paths crossed today. That the world contains people like this.

[20s pause]

Now your body. Your body has been working since you woke up — since before you woke up — and you probably haven't thanked it once. We treat our bodies like machines, noticing them only when they break. But your body has been carrying you faithfully all morning.

It digested your food. It carried you from room to room. It processed a thousand sensory signals every second without asking your permission. Your heart has been beating — tens of thousands of times since you woke up. You didn't have to ask. You didn't have to remember. It just... kept going. The most loyal thing in your life, and the one you're least grateful for.

[15s pause]

Place your attention on one part of your body that's working well right now. Not a part that hurts or frustrates you — a part that's quietly, faithfully functioning. Your eyes, reading or resting behind closed lids. Your lungs, pulling in air. Your hands, which can still grip and hold and touch and feel. Your legs, that carried you this morning. Your ears, catching every sound.

[15s pause]

Feel it working. Feel the quiet miracle of function — of a body that hasn't given up on you despite everything you put it through. The late nights and the skipped meals and the stress you carry in your shoulders. It's still here. Still beating. Still breathing.

Silently: thank you.

[15s pause]

Paul writes to the Thessalonians: "Give thanks in all circumstances; for this is God's will for you in Christ Jesus." First Thessalonians five, verse eighteen.

In all circumstances. Not for all circumstances — that's an important difference, and it matters. Paul isn't asking you to be grateful for pain, for loss, for injustice, for the thing that broke your heart. He's not asking you to pretend the hard things aren't hard. He's inviting you to find gratitude in the midst of whatever is happening. Inside the difficulty. Alongside the sorrow. Not instead of it — in it. Even the hard days hold something worth noticing, if you look.

[15s pause]

So here's the harder layer. And you don't have to force this — if it's not there, it's not there, and that's honest. But look.

Think back over the morning. Was there a difficult moment? A frustration. A disappointment. A conversation that didn't go the way you hoped. A wave of anxiety that hit you at your desk. A pang of loneliness in a crowded room. A task that felt pointless. An interaction that left you drained.

Don't push it away. Let it come forward. You're not going to dwell in it — just acknowledge it. What was the hard thing?

[20s pause]

Ignatius would call this a desolation — a moment where your heart contracted, where you felt further from life, from God, from joy. It's real. It counts. Don't pretend it didn't happen.

[15s pause]

Now — this is the counterintuitive part, the part that Ignatius believed was most important — is there anything in that difficult moment to be grateful for? Not grateful for the pain itself. Never that. But grateful for something the difficulty revealed.

Maybe it showed you what you care about — because you only hurt over things that matter. Maybe it showed you your own strength — that you made it through, that you kept going, that you didn't quit even when you wanted to. Maybe it showed you your need for God — the way thirst shows you your need for water. Maybe it simply showed you that you are alive, that you are someone who feels things deeply, and that this capacity to feel is itself a gift, even when it hurts.

[20s pause]

Look for the grace hidden in the hard thing. It might be small. It might be hiding in the corner of the room. It might take a moment to find. But Ignatius trusted that God is present even in the desolation — maybe especially in the desolation. Not causing it. But inhabiting it. Meeting you there.

[15s pause]

If you found something, hold it gently. If you didn't, that's okay too. Not every hard thing yields its grace on schedule. Some take weeks. Some take years. Some you may never understand. That's honest. God can work with honest.

Silently, if you can — and only if it's true: thank you. Even for this. Even here.

[20s pause]

Now let's gather all of it. The small good moment. The person. The body. The difficult thing. Let them sit together — not sorted into categories, not ranked by importance. Just... the morning. The whole morning.

This is your day so far. This is the day the Lord has made — not the sanitized version, not the highlight reel, not the curated memory. The real one. The one with coffee and frustration and grace and exhaustion and kindness and a body that kept breathing through all of it.

[15s pause]

Where does gratitude live in your body right now? Can you feel it? It might be a warmth in your chest — a spreading, gentle heat. A lightness in your limbs. A prickling behind the eyes that might or might not become tears. Or it might be quieter than all of that — just a stillness, a settling, a sense of something being okay even though not everything is okay. An exhale that goes a little deeper than usual.

Whatever it is, stay with it. Let it fill the space.

[20s pause]

"Let us rejoice and be glad in it."

Not after we've fixed everything. Not once the anxiety passes. Not when the to-do list is done, because it's never done. Not when we finally have the life we think we're working toward. In it. Now. In this imperfect, interrupted, messy, ordinary day. This is the day. The only one you've got right now. And joy is available in it — not the giddy, everything-is-great kind. The deep kind. The kind that coexists with difficulty. The kind that notices grace in the middle of a hard morning and says: yes. Even here. Even now.

[20s pause]

Let me pray with you.

God, thank you for this day. The real one — not the one I planned, not the one I wished for, not the one I'm still hoping will magically materialize after lunch. The one I actually got. This one. With all of it.

Thank you for the good in it. The small moments I almost missed because I was rushing. The person who made it less lonely. The body that carried me here, still beating, still breathing, still somehow working after everything I ask of it.

And thank you — though I say this with some hesitation, with an honest lump in my throat — for the hard parts too. Not because I enjoyed them. Not because they were good. But because even in the difficulty, you were there. Even when I didn't notice. Even when I couldn't feel it. You were there.

Teach me to pay attention. Not just in this meditation, not just in the quiet moments, but in the noise and the rush and the ordinary middle of a day that doesn't feel special but is. Help me catch the next gift before it passes. Help me notice the next person before I forget to be grateful. Help me see the burning bush in the boring meeting, the grace in the grocery line, the presence of God in the place I least expect it.

And when this day ends, let me look back and see — not a day wasted, not a day merely survived, but a day lived. A day full of ordinary, sacred things I almost overlooked but didn't. Because I paused. Because I looked. Because you taught me to see.

Thank you. For all of it. For this breath, right now, which I did not earn and do not deserve and cannot explain. Thank you.

Amen.

Not because optimism is a virtue or because positivity fixes everything. But because attention is a form of prayer. And you just prayed. And it was honest. And it was real.

This is the day the Lord has made. It matters more than you think.
