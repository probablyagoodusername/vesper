Now... whose voice is that, really? A parent? A teacher? A coach? A past version of yourself? Culture? The internet? It has a source. And that source is not God.

Paul writes to the Corinthians: "Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, the new creation has come. The old has gone, the new is here." Second Corinthians five, seventeen.

[10s pause]

New creation. Not a repaired version of the old. Not the same broken thing with a fresh coat of paint. Something new. Something that didn't exist before.

The inner critic speaks from the old creation. It references the old data. The old failures, the old labels, the old metrics. It doesn't know about the new thing. It can't see it, because it's looking in the wrong direction.

[15s pause]

And to the Galatians, Paul writes something even more startling: "I have been crucified with Christ and I no longer live, but Christ lives in me. The life I now live in the body, I live by faith in the Son of God, who loved me and gave himself for me." Galatians two, twenty.

[10s pause]

Your deepest identity is not your performance. Not your appearance. Not your productivity. Not your worst moment or your best. It is this: Christ lives in you. The Spirit of the living God has made a home in your body. You are indwelt. Inhabited. Not by an idea of love — by a person.

[20s pause]

This is not denial. The struggles are real. The pain is real. The critic will keep talking — maybe for the rest of your life. But the identity beneath the struggle is secure. It does not depend on your ability to silence the critic. It does not depend on your performance today or tomorrow. It was settled before you did anything to earn it or lose it.

Father... we bring you the voices that have told us who we are. The critical ones. The shaming ones. The ones we've memorized. We don't ask you to shout over them. We ask you to speak beneath them. In the quiet place under the noise.

Remind us. Not once but daily. That our identity is not up for debate. That we are new creations. That Christ lives in us — not as a metaphor but as a reality. That we are yours before we are anything else.
