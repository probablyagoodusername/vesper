Tonight we're going to practice something ancient. For more than fifteen hundred years — since the early monasteries of the Benedictine tradition — Christians have practiced what they call Lectio Divina. Sacred reading. It wasn't invented as a technique or a spiritual exercise. It was simply the way monks received scripture — slowly, prayerfully, with the expectation that God would speak through it. Not information to be mastered, but a living word to be encountered.

Saint Benedict taught his monks to listen "with the ear of the heart." That's what we're doing tonight. Not studying. Not analyzing. Listening — for a word that might be meant specifically for you.

The practice has four movements. Lectio — reading. Meditatio — meditating. Oratio — praying. And Contemplatio — resting in God's presence. You don't have to remember the names. Just follow the movements. They'll carry you.

[15s pause]

We begin with Lectio. Reading. Or rather, listening — because in the monasteries, scripture was always read aloud. The ear receives differently than the eye. Something that passes quickly on a page might stop you in your tracks when you hear it spoken.

I'm going to read a passage from Psalm twenty-seven. The first time through, simply receive the words. Don't try to catch everything. Don't analyze. Don't study. Just let the words wash over you the way water washes over stone.

[30s pause]

Now we move to Oratio. Prayer. This is your response — the word has touched something in you, and now you speak back. Not to yourself. To God. About what's been stirred, what's been uncovered, what's been found.

You don't need eloquent words. You don't need theology or proper form. Just honesty. If the word stirred grief, tell him. If it stirred longing, say so. If it stirred confusion — "I don't know what this means, but something in me responded" — that's a prayer. If you're not sure what you feel, say that. God can work with honesty. He can't work with performance.

[15s pause]

Speak to him now. In your own words. Or without words — just a feeling, a gesture of the heart, an inner turning toward the One who gave you this word tonight.

[30s pause]

Take your time. There's no formula. Just you and God and the word between you.

[30s pause]

If there's more to say, say it. If the prayer has already become silence, let it be silence. Silence between friends isn't awkward. It's intimate.

[30s pause]

And now we enter the final movement. Contemplatio. Contemplation. This is the simplest and the hardest part, because you're asked to let go of everything — the word, the meditation, the feelings, even the prayer. Everything.

You're not doing anything now. You're not thinking, analyzing, asking, responding, or working. You're simply resting. Present to God, who is present to you.

The contemplatives called this "resting in God." It's what happens when the conversation gives way to presence. When two people who love each other have said what needs to be said, and now they're just... together. In comfortable silence. The kind of silence that holds more than words ever could.

Every return is the prayer. Every time you notice you've drifted and choose to come back — that is the practice. That is the love.
