This is how many of us move through life. Gripping. Holding on. Trying to control the outcome, manage the situation, prevent the thing we're afraid of. It's exhausting. And it doesn't work.

[15s pause]

[15s pause]

Feel the difference. The open hand and the clenched fist are not just different postures — they are different ways of being in the world. One says: "I have to hold this together." The other says: "I am held."

Can any one of you by worrying add a single hour to your life?

The answer is no. We know this. And yet we keep worrying — because worry feels like doing something. It feels productive. It tricks us into thinking that if we just think about it hard enough, anticipate every possible failure, run every scenario... we can prevent the bad thing from happening.

We can't. Control is an illusion. And it costs us everything — our sleep, our peace, our presence, our joy. We spend so much energy holding the world together that we have nothing left for actually living in it.

Proverbs three, verses five and six: "Trust in the Lord with all your heart and lean not on your own understanding; in all your ways submit to him, and he will make your paths straight."

[10s pause]

Lean not on your own understanding. That's the hard part, isn't it? Because our understanding is all we have. Our analysis, our planning, our predicting. And the verse says: don't lean on that. Lean on something else.

This is not about being passive. It's not about doing nothing and hoping for the best. It's about redirecting trust. From yourself — from your capacity to manage every variable — to God. Who sees what you can't. Who holds what you can't. Who is already in the future you're trying to control.

I want you to take the thing you named earlier — the thing you're gripping — and imagine placing it in your open hands. Not throwing it away. Not pretending it doesn't matter. Placing it there. Gently. Like something valuable that you're choosing to stop clutching.

[15s pause]

Feel the weight of it in your open palms. And then... offer it upward. Not with force. With willingness. A quiet gesture that says: "I cannot carry this alone anymore. I was never supposed to."

[20s pause]

This is surrender. And surrender is terrifying. Because it means admitting that you are not in control. That you cannot guarantee the outcome. That the thing you love most in the world might not go the way you planned.

Let that fear be here. Don't push it away. Surrender that isn't afraid isn't surrender — it's indifference. Real surrender feels like stepping off a ledge. And trusting that the ground will meet you.

[25s pause]

Feel your open hands. Feel the vulnerability of that position. Palms up. Undefended. This is not weakness. This is the most courageous posture a human being can take — to stop pretending you're in charge.

The Christian tradition has long practiced what some call "the prayer of relinquishment." It's simple. You hold your hands open before God and say: "Into your hands."

That's it. Not a long prayer. Not a sophisticated theology. Just... into your hands.

[20s pause]

This doesn't mean the worry disappears. You may close your hands again in ten minutes, an hour, tomorrow morning. That's fine. That's human. The practice is not achieving permanent surrender. The practice is noticing when you're gripping — and opening again. And again. And again. As many times as it takes.

Each opening is an act of trust. Each opening is a prayer.
