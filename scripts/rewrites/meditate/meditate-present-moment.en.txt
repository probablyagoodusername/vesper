[BREATHING: Deep calm breathing — 5 seconds in through the nose, 7 seconds out through the mouth. Guide at opening. Natural rhythm thereafter.]

Let's begin simply. You're here. That's enough for now.

Take a breath. A real one — not the shallow, automatic kind you've been taking all day. A slow breath in through your nose... two... three... four... five.

And out through your mouth... two... three... four... five... six... seven.

[5s pause]

Again. In... letting your chest expand, your belly soften, your ribs widen... four... five.

And out... releasing, like setting down something heavy... four... five... six... seven.

[5s pause]

Once more. In... slowly, filling all the way.

And out... all the way to the bottom. Emptying.

[10s pause]

Let your breathing settle into whatever rhythm it finds. Don't control it anymore. Just watch it for a moment, the way you'd watch waves from a shore. The rise. The fall. The tiny pause between. Your body has been breathing all day without your permission. It doesn't need you to manage this.

[15s pause]

Now feel your body. Not an idea of your body — not a mental image of yourself — the actual, physical, sensory fact of you, sitting here, right now.

Start with your feet. Feel the soles of your feet. The pressure against the floor, or the inside of your shoes, or the surface beneath you. The weight. The temperature. The contact.

[15s pause]

Now your legs. The heaviness of them. The way they're positioned. The muscles — are they tense? Relaxed? Somewhere in between?

[10s pause]

Your hands. Where are they resting? What do they feel? The texture of whatever they're touching. The temperature. The pulse in your fingertips.

[10s pause]

Your stomach. Rising and falling with the breath. Soft or tight. Full or empty.

[10s pause]

Your chest. The heartbeat you didn't ask for. The lungs expanding and contracting on their own schedule. The warmth.

[10s pause]

Your face. Your jaw — is it clenched? Let it soften. Your eyes, resting behind their lids. Your forehead — smooth it, gently.

[10s pause]

Feel gravity holding you here. Feel the solidness of the surface beneath you. You are a body, in a place, at a particular moment in time. This is not a concept. This is a fact. And right now, this fact is the only thing that matters.

[15s pause]

How much of your life do you spend somewhere other than here? Be honest. Replaying yesterday — the conversation you wish had gone differently, the mistake you keep revisiting. Rehearsing tomorrow — running through scenarios, planning responses to situations that haven't happened, preparing for disasters that may never arrive.

Your body is always in the present. Always. It doesn't know how to be anywhere else. It's your mind that time-travels — back and forward, back and forward — while your body sits patiently, waiting for you to come home.

[15s pause]

There's a line from Jesus that gets quoted often but rarely understood on its own terms. "Therefore do not worry about tomorrow, for tomorrow will worry about itself. Each day has enough trouble of its own." Matthew six, verse thirty-four.

We hear that and think it means "don't plan ahead" or "be irresponsible about the future." But that's not what he's saying. He's saying: stop living in a tomorrow that doesn't exist yet. Come back to today. There's enough here — enough trouble, yes, but also enough grace, enough beauty, enough God — to fill this moment completely. You don't need to borrow from tomorrow.

[20s pause]

A seventeenth-century monk named Brother Lawrence spent most of his life working in the kitchen of a Carmelite monastery in Paris. Washing dishes. Repairing sandals. Peeling vegetables. The most unglamorous, invisible, repetitive work imaginable. And yet he described those years as the most profound spiritual practice of his life.

He called it "the practice of the presence of God."

His insight was disarmingly simple. God is not more present in the chapel than in the kitchen. God is not more available during prayer than during dishes. The sacred is not somewhere else — some holy place, some spiritual mountaintop, some future version of your life where you finally have time to be devout. The sacred is here. In this room. In this breath. In this ordinary, unremarkable, Tuesday-afternoon moment.

[15s pause]

Think of Moses and the burning bush. It was a bush — an ordinary, scrubby plant on the side of a road in the desert. Nothing special. Except that God was in it. And the miracle wasn't really the fire. The miracle was that Moses stopped. He turned aside. He paid attention.

The present moment is the burning bush. It's where God is. Always has been. The question isn't whether God is here. The question is whether you'll stop long enough to notice.

[20s pause]

So let's practice noticing. Right now.

What do you hear? Don't label the sounds as pleasant or annoying, wanted or unwanted. Just... what is reaching your ears? The hum of something mechanical. A distant voice. Wind. Traffic. Birdsong. Silence itself, which has its own sound if you listen closely enough. Whatever it is — receive it. It belongs to this moment.

[20s pause]

What do you feel on your skin? The temperature of the air. A slight draft. The texture of your clothing against your arms, your legs. The weight of your own hands resting. Can you feel the air moving in and out of your nostrils — the slight coolness on the inhale, the warmth on the exhale?

[15s pause]

What does the air taste like, smell like? This is a sense we almost never pay attention to. But it's here, constant, carrying quiet information about where you are, what season it is, what time of day.

[15s pause]

This is presence. Not an exotic state of consciousness. Not something monks achieve after decades of practice. Just... paying attention to what is actually happening, right now, in your body, in this place. It's shockingly simple. And shockingly rare.

[15s pause]

Now let's go deeper. We're going to use a practice from the centering prayer tradition — a way of being present to God that the Christian contemplatives have taught for centuries.

Choose a sacred word. A single word that expresses your desire to be present to God in this moment. It might be "here." It might be "peace." It might be "Jesus." It might be "rest." Or "mercy." Or "love." Or "come." Whatever rises naturally in you. Don't overthink it. Let it arrive.

[15s pause]

Hold that word gently, like holding a candle in cupped hands. It's not a mantra you're chanting. It's not a magic formula. It's more like an anchor — a way of returning when your mind drifts. And your mind will drift. That's not failure. That's not weakness. That's just what minds do.

The practice isn't maintaining perfect, unbroken focus — no one does that. The practice is returning. Every single time you notice you've wandered and gently come back to your sacred word — that return is the practice. That is the prayer. Not the stillness. The returning.

[15s pause]

Let's begin. Settle into silence. When thoughts come — and they will, immediately, a whole parade of them — don't fight them. Don't be angry at your brain for thinking. Just notice what's happening. You might silently name it: "planning." "Remembering." "Worrying." "Judging." "Wandering." Just a soft label. And then gently, without frustration, return to your sacred word.

Each return is an act of trust. Each return says: I choose this moment over the imagined one. I choose presence over distraction. I choose the burning bush over the road.

[15s pause]

Begin now. Rest in the silence. Return to your word as often as you need to.

[60s pause]

If you've been pulled away, that's fine. Come back now. No judgment. Just your word. Just this breath. Just here.

[60s pause]

Stay a little longer. This is the deep end of the practice, and it might feel uncomfortable. It might feel boring. It might feel like nothing is happening — like you're wasting time, sitting here doing nothing when you could be doing something productive.

But something is happening. Beneath the noise, beneath the restlessness, beneath the mind's insistence that you should be elsewhere doing something useful — you are consenting to God's presence. You are saying yes to being here. And that consent... that is the whole prayer. Not the perfect silence. Not the mystical experience. Just the willingness to stay.

[60s pause]

[15s pause]

Now, gently, let the formal practice go. You don't need to hold the sacred word anymore. Let it dissolve, like a word written in sand. But stay present. Stay in your body.

[10s pause]

Feel your feet again. Really feel them — the soles, the toes, the arches. What's the sensation? Warmth? Pressure? Tingling?

[10s pause]

Feel your hands. Open your fingers slightly. Notice the air between them. The weight of each finger.

[10s pause]

Feel your chest. The heartbeat that hasn't paused once during this entire practice. The breath you didn't have to earn. Your body has been here the whole time — faithful, patient, keeping you alive while your mind traveled to a hundred different places and back.

[15s pause]

A French Jesuit named Jean-Pierre de Caussade wrote about what he called "the sacrament of the present moment." He believed that God's will is not hidden in some distant future you have to decode. It's not a puzzle to solve, a career to build, a destination to reach. It's here. Right here. In this moment, in whatever is in front of you.

The next conversation you have. The next task you sit down to. The next meal. The next breath. Each one is an encounter with the living God, if you have eyes to see it and the stillness to notice.

You don't have to go looking for God. You never did. God is already in the room. In the breath you just took. In the heartbeat you didn't request. In the ordinary, unremarkable present that your mind keeps trying to escape.

[20s pause]

"Each day has enough trouble of its own."

True. But each day has enough beauty of its own too. Enough grace. Enough surprise. Enough breath. You're alive in this moment. You're breathing, conscious, present. That is not small. That is the burning bush — alight with the presence of God, waiting for you to turn aside and see.

[20s pause]

Let me pray.

God, I confess that I spend most of my life somewhere else. Rehearsing the future. Regretting the past. Running from the present because it's too ordinary, too uncomfortable, too boring to contain you. And yet you say you're here. You say the bush is always burning. You say every moment is sacred ground if I'd only take off my shoes and pay attention.

Teach me to be where I am. To see the holy in the ordinary — in the kitchen, on the commute, in the conversation I'd rather avoid, in the waiting room, in the line, in the silence I keep trying to fill with noise. Not just in the mountaintop moments. In the Tuesday-afternoon ones too.

Help me trust that this moment is enough. That you are in it. That I don't need to be somewhere else, someone else, in some other season of life to find you. You're here. You've always been here.

Thank you for Brother Lawrence and his dishes. Thank you for Moses and his bush. Thank you for every contemplative who sat still long enough to discover that the sacred isn't somewhere else — it's right where they were.

Help me learn what they learned. Slowly, imperfectly, one returned breath at a time.

Amen.

[20s pause]

As you prepare to return to your day, take one more breath. Slow. Full. All the way in. All the way out.

[10s pause]

Feel your body one last time. Solid. Present. Here. The weight of you on this surface. The aliveness in your fingers and toes. The steady, given rhythm of your heart.

[10s pause]

As you move back into the world, carry this with you — not as a burden, not as an obligation, but as a gentle intention. For the next hour, notice one thing you would normally miss. One thing. A color. A sound. A face. A taste. A shadow. A breeze.

One burning bush, hiding in plain sight. It's there. It's always there.

That's the practice. Not perfect, unbroken presence — no one lives like that this side of heaven. Just one more moment of attention than you had yesterday. One more turning aside to see.
