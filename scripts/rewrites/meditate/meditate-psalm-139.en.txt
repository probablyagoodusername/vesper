We're going to sit with Psalm one thirty-nine today. This is one of the most intimate passages in all of scripture. It's a psalm about being known — completely, entirely, without remainder. And that... can be both comforting and terrifying. Because most of us spend a great deal of energy making sure we are not fully known.

[10s pause]

"You have searched me, Lord, and you know me."

[10s pause]

Sit with that verb. Searched. Not glanced at. Not skimmed. Searched. The way you'd search for something precious that you lost. With attention. With care. With intent.

And the result of that searching is not a verdict. Not a sentence. Not a disappointed sigh. The result is... you know me.

Not "you have judged me." Not "you have found me wanting." You have searched me — and you know me. All of me. And you stayed.

[20s pause]

"You know when I sit and when I rise; you perceive my thoughts from afar."

Every thought you're ashamed of. Every fear you've never spoken out loud. Every hope that feels too fragile to name. Known. Perceived. Not condemned.

[15s pause]

Notice what happens in your body when you hear that. Does something relax? Or does something tighten? Both responses are honest. Some of us long to be known. Some of us are terrified of it. Some of us are both at the same time.

Whatever you feel — let it be there. Don't fix it. Just notice it.

[20s pause]

"You hem me in behind and before, and you lay your hand upon me."

Feel that image in your body. Hemmed in — not trapped, but held. Behind you: your past is held. Every version of yourself you've been, every mistake, every wound. Before you: your future is held. The unknown, the uncertain, the things that keep you awake. And upon you: a hand. Right now. Not pushing. Not pulling. Resting.

[20s pause]

Now... before we go further into this psalm, I want to pause here. Because there's something we need to be honest about first.

Many of us carry a deep, quiet belief that if God really searched us — really looked — what he'd find would be disappointing. Or disgusting. Or not enough.

There is a voice in you that has been narrating your story for a long time. It says things like: you're too much. Or not enough. Or broken in a way that can't be repaired. Or falling behind. Or fundamentally flawed.

[10s pause]

Where does that voice live in your body right now? Is it a tightness in your chest? A heaviness in your stomach? A constriction in your throat? A kind of shrinking feeling, like you want to take up less space?

Find it. Don't push it away. Just locate it.

[20s pause]

This is shame. And shame tells us we are the problem. Not that we made a mistake — that we are a mistake. It's the oldest lie in scripture. After the fall, Adam and Eve hid. Not because they'd broken a rule, but because they believed they'd become something God wouldn't want to look at.

And here is God's response. He didn't turn away. He came looking. "Where are you?"

[15s pause]

He's still asking. Not because he doesn't know. Because he wants you to come out of hiding.

Now... we're going to practice something. This is a self-compassion exercise rooted in the work of Kristin Neff, and it has three parts. Each part is grounded in what this psalm teaches. Stay with me.

The first part is mindfulness — simply acknowledging what is painful, without minimizing it or drowning in it.

Place a hand on your heart. Or both hands, if that feels right. Feel the warmth of your palm against your chest.

[10s pause]

Now, inwardly, name what hurts. Not a long story. Just the ache itself. "This is hard." Or "I'm struggling." Or "I'm carrying something heavy."

Say it to yourself the way you would say it to someone you love.

[20s pause]

The second part is common humanity. Shame whispers that you are uniquely broken. That everyone else has it together and you are the exception. But Psalm one thirty-nine was written by a person. A real, struggling, sometimes desperate person. David — who failed spectacularly and publicly. Who wrote this psalm not from a place of having it all figured out, but from the middle of being human.

So say this inwardly: "I am not alone in this. Struggle is part of being human. Others carry this weight too."

[20s pause]

Feel the truth of that. You are not the only one. You never were.

[15s pause]

The third part is self-kindness. This is the hardest one for most people. We know how to be kind to others. Turning that same warmth toward ourselves feels... indulgent. Or dishonest. But scripture does not ask you to be your own harshest critic. It asks you to receive the kindness God extends.

With your hand still on your heart, say this inwardly: "May I be kind to myself. May I give myself the compassion I need."

[15s pause]

If those words feel foreign... that's okay. You're not performing. You're practicing receiving.

Now — back to the psalm. Listen to this:

"I praise you because I am fearfully and wonderfully made; your works are wonderful, I know that full well."

[10s pause]

Let's sit with this. Not as a motivational quote on a coffee mug. As a declaration about your specific body. Your specific mind. Your specific story.

The things you wish were different about yourself — your body shape, your way of thinking, your emotional sensitivity, your limitations — what if they were not accidents?

[25s pause]

"Wonderfully made" does not mean "without struggle." It does not mean everything is easy or painless or fair. It means: crafted with intention. Deliberately. On purpose. Every fiber. Every neural pathway. Every scar.

Paul writes in Ephesians: "We are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do." Ephesians two, verse ten. The Greek word there — poiema, poy-AY-mah — is where we get the word "poem." You are not a rough draft. You are not an assembly-line product. You are a crafted thing. A poem.

[20s pause]

"How precious to me are your thoughts, God! How vast is the sum of them! Were I to count them, they would outnumber the grains of sand."

God thinks about you more than there are grains of sand on every beach you've ever walked. Not thoughts of disappointment. Not thoughts of frustration. Thoughts of tenderness. Thoughts of delight.

[20s pause]

Let that land somewhere in your body. Not just your mind. Your body. Where does delight live? Can you feel even a flicker of it? A warmth in your chest, a softening around your eyes, a releasing in your belly?

If you can't feel it — that's okay too. Sometimes the body needs time to catch up with what the mind knows. You don't have to force anything.
