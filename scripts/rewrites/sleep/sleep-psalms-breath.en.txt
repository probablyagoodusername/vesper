Settle into your position for sleep. Take a moment to adjust anything that needs adjusting. Your pillow — higher, lower, flatter, whatever feels right. Your blanket — pulled up or pushed down. The way your body lies against the mattress. Make yourself comfortable. Make yourself at home in this bed.

[10s pause]

Notice the points where your body makes contact with the surface beneath you. The back of your head, cradled by the pillow. Your shoulder blades pressing gently into the mattress. Your lower back. The backs of your arms. Your calves. Your heels. Wherever you feel contact, let your weight sink a little more into that place. Let the mattress receive you. It's been waiting all day for this.

[10s pause]

Let your eyes close. Let your jaw soften — let it hang open just slightly, so your teeth aren't touching. Let your tongue drop from the roof of your mouth. Let your hands fall open at your sides. Let your fingers uncurl.

Tonight, you don't need to do anything. You don't need to solve anything. You don't need to plan or review or decide. You just need to breathe. And even that, your body will do for you.

[10s pause]

Take one deep, unstructured breath in through your nose... fill your whole chest and belly... and release it slowly through your mouth with a long sigh.

[5s pause]

[5s pause]

One more. Deep breath in... and let everything ride out on the exhale. Good.

Tonight we breathe with the Psalms. The Psalms are the oldest prayers in the faith — they've been on human lips for three thousand years. Whispered in temples, chanted in cathedrals, cried out in desert nights, murmured in hospital rooms. They are honest prayers. They don't pretend everything is fine. They rage and weep and doubt and praise and beg and surrender. And tonight, three of them will become the rhythm of your breath.

We'll use a practice called coherent breathing — a slow, even rhythm that research has shown calms the autonomic nervous system and brings the heart, lungs, and brain into sync. About five and a half seconds in, five and a half seconds out. You don't need to count precisely. I'll pace you with the words, and your body will find the rhythm.

Our first psalm phrase comes from Psalm sixty-two. David, running from enemies, hunted and afraid, writes this: "Truly my soul finds rest in God alone."

Not in safety. Not in certainty. Not in having everything figured out. In God alone.

We'll split the verse across the breath. The first half rides the inhale. The second half rides the exhale. Let the words become the breath. Let the breath become the prayer.

Our next phrase comes from Psalm one hundred and thirty-one. It's one of the shortest psalms — only three verses. And one of the most tender. The psalmist writes: "I have calmed and quieted my soul; like a weaned child with its mother, like a weaned child is my soul within me."

A weaned child. Think about what that means. Not a hungry infant, crying for milk, needing something urgently. A child who has been fed. Who has no craving left. Who simply rests against its mother's chest because it wants to be near her. Content. Still. At home. Wanting nothing except what it already has.

Be still. Not "be productive." Not "be ready." Not "figure it out." Be still. The Hebrew word is raphah — it means to let go, to release your grip, to stop striving. It's a command to do... nothing. The only command in the Bible that asks less of you, not more.
