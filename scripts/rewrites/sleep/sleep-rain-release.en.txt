Tonight, we make room for it. Not to fix it. Not to analyze it until it makes sense. Not to make it go away through force of will. Just to be with it honestly. And to discover that being with it is not as dangerous as it seems.

Tonight we practice something called RAIN. It's a framework from the contemplative psychology tradition, adapted beautifully for moments like this one — moments when something is present in you that won't quiet down on its own. Four steps. Recognize. Allow. Investigate. Nurture. We'll take each one slowly, with more time than you might expect, because these things can't be rushed. The feeling took all day to arrive. We can give it more than thirty seconds.

[10s pause]

Recognize.

The first step is simply noticing what's here. Not what you think should be here. Not what would be reasonable to feel. Not what you wish you felt. What is actually present, right now, in this body, in this bed, at this hour.

Scan your body gently. Start at the top of your head and move downward... slowly... like lowering a lantern into a well. Notice wherever something catches the light. Is there tightness in your forehead — a band of pressure across the brow? A clenching in your jaw that came back even after you released it? A knot between your shoulder blades, as though the muscles there are still bracing for something?

[15s pause]

Move lower. Is there heaviness in your chest... a weight that doesn't correspond to anything physical? A tightness in the solar plexus, that soft spot below the ribs? A churning in your stomach, or a hollowness? A restlessness in your legs that makes you want to move, to get up, to pace?

[15s pause]

Whatever you find... wherever the lantern catches something... pause there. That's information. The body stores what the mind doesn't process. You're reading the residue of the day, the week, maybe longer. The body has been holding this for you. It's been waiting for you to notice.

[15s pause]

Now, try to name the feeling underneath the sensation. Not the story — not the reason or the cause or whose fault it is. Just the feeling itself. One or two words.

Anxious. Sad. Frustrated. Ashamed. Lonely. Overwhelmed. Angry. Numb. Disappointed. Afraid. Hurt. Tired in a way that has nothing to do with sleep.

[15s pause]

Whatever word arrived, even if it surprised you, even if it doesn't fully capture the thing... that's close enough. You've recognized it. You've turned toward it instead of away. That is the first step, and it takes more courage than most people realize. Most people fall asleep every night without ever asking themselves what they're feeling. You asked.

[15s pause]

Allow.

This is the step where most of us get stuck, because everything in us wants to fix, change, or escape difficult feelings. The habit is deep. When something hurts, we push it away. When something is uncomfortable, we distract ourselves. When something feels wrong, we try to make it right. But tonight... just for this practice... we're going to try something different.

Instead of pushing this feeling away, or analyzing it, or bargaining with it, or shaming yourself for having it... can you simply allow it to be here? Not forever. Not as a permanent resident. Just for now. Just for these few minutes.

Say silently to yourself: This is here. I don't have to fight it.

[15s pause]

Allowing doesn't mean approving. This is important. Allowing doesn't mean you want this feeling, or that it's right, or that it deserves to stay, or that it gets the final word about who you are. It means you stop fighting what is already here. You stop spending energy pushing against something that is already inside you. That fight is exhausting. It burns energy you need for sleep, for healing, for tomorrow. When you stop the fight... something in the chest loosens. Something in the shoulders drops.

[15s pause]

Notice what happens in your body when you stop resisting. Does your breathing deepen slightly? Does your jaw unclench again? Does the feeling itself shift, even a little... becoming less sharp, less urgent, now that it's not being attacked?

[15s pause]

Investigate.

The way you'd approach a friend who is hurting... sitting down beside them, not demanding they talk, just being close enough that they could.

If at any point this feels too intense, return your attention to the sensation of breathing. The weight of your body on the bed. The feeling of your head on the pillow. You can always come back to this later. That's not failure — that's wisdom.

Not "you're amazing and everything is fine." Kind in the way that actually helps... which is honest, and tender, and close.
